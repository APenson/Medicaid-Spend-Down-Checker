Medicaid Eligibility Limits Quick Reference (2024)

Typical Asset Limits (varies by state):

  • Single Individual: $2,000 - $2,742 in countable assets
  • Married Couple (both applying): $3,000 - $4,113 in countable assets
  • Community Spouse (when one spouse applies): May retain $29,724 - $148,620
  • Home Equity Limit: $713,000 - $1,071,000 (varies by state)

Exempt (Non-Countable) Assets Generally Include:

  • Primary residence (up to equity limits, if returning home is possible)
  • One vehicle (used for transportation)
  • Personal belongings and household items
  • Burial spaces and up to $1,500 in burial funds per person
  • Life insurance with face value under $1,500
  • Income-producing property essential for support

Countable Assets Generally Include:

  • Cash, checking and savings accounts
  • Certificates of deposit (CDs)
  • Stocks, bonds, mutual funds
  • Second homes or rental properties
  • Additional vehicles
  • Valuable collections (art, jewelry, etc.)

Medicaid Spend Down Strategies (Educational Overview)

Allowable Spend Down Options May Include:

  • Medical expenses: Uncovered medical bills, dental work, vision care
  • Home modifications: Accessibility improvements, ramps, grab bars
  • Prepaid burial expenses: Funeral and burial arrangements
  • Home repairs: Necessary maintenance and improvements
  • Debt payment: Credit cards, loans, outstanding bills
  • Vehicle purchase: Reliable transportation for spouse/family
  • Household items: Necessary furniture, appliances

Important Lookback Period Warning:

Medicaid has a 5-year lookback period for most asset transfers. Transfers made within 5 years of applying may result in a penalty period where you're ineligible for benefits. Always consult with an elder law attorney before making significant financial changes.

What Does NOT Count as Spend Down:

  • Gifts to family members (may create penalties)
  • Transfers for less than fair market value
  • Hiding or concealing assets
  • Creating artificial expenses
  • Luxury purchases that don't benefit the applicant

State-Specific Considerations

Medicaid Expansion vs. Non-Expansion States:

Expansion States: Generally have higher income limits and may offer more home and community-based services.

Non-Expansion States: May have stricter eligibility requirements and fewer community-based options.

Special State Programs to Research:

  • Miller Trusts: Income trusts for states with strict income limits
  • PACE Programs: Programs of All-Inclusive Care for the Elderly
  • 1915(c) Waivers: Home and Community-Based Services waivers
  • State-specific asset limits: Some states have higher limits than federal minimums
